How this guide works

Read slow. Follow steps. Copy examples in your own topic.
Idea
A proposal is a plan, not the full study
You are not writing results. You are not writing a full thesis. You are telling others what you want to study, why it matters, and how you will do it. Think of it like a building plan before you build a house.
If you feel you "do not know enough yet", that is normal. The proposal shows that you understand the problem and have a clear, honest plan.
Who reads it
Your reader’s secret checklist
Your reader silently checks:
  • Is this problem worth time and money?
  • Is the plan clear and realistic?
  • Can this person really do it?
Your job is to make each answer an easy “yes”.

Basics

What a research proposal really is (in plain words)

One sentence, then deeper, then example.
Short meaning
A research proposal is a written plan that:
  • Shows the problem you want to study.
  • Explains why this problem is important.
  • Describes how you will study it.
  • States how long it will take and what it will cost.
Think: "Here is the problem. Here is my way to solve it. Here is why I am able to do it."
Tiny everyday example
Imagine you ask your department for money to run a workshop. You send a note that says:
  • What workshop is about (problem).
  • Why students need it (importance).
  • How you will run it (method).
  • How much money and time it needs (cost and time).
That note is a small proposal. A research proposal is the same idea, but in more depth and with more care.
PhD-level example
Example topic: “Phone addiction & sleep in first-year students”
If this is your topic, your proposal must answer:
  • Problem: “Many first-year students use phones late at night. Sleep may be poor.”
  • Why it matters: “Poor sleep can hurt grades and mental health.”
  • Plan: “We will measure phone use and sleep quality with a survey and sleep logs.”
  • Cost: “We need money for survey tools, small gift cards, and data software.”
  • Time: “Study will run over 9 months: design, collect data, analyze, write.”

Big picture

Different types of research proposals

Use the right “shape” so your reader is not confused.

There are several common types of proposals. You do not need to use all of them. You just need to know which one fits your case. Here they are with simple examples.

Inside your own place
Internal proposal
Meaning: Sent inside your own university or department. Often for small changes or internal funds.
Example

You ask your department for a small grant to buy software for your PhD study. You send a proposal to your Head of Department only.

Outside funder
External proposal
Meaning: Sent to another body or agency. For example, a research council, charity, or industry partner.
Example

You send a proposal to a national science council to fund your 3-year PhD project on climate-smart farming.

By request
Solicited proposal
Meaning: Someone asks for it in a formal call. You respond to a document like “Request for Proposals (RFP)”.
Example

A health agency releases a call: “We want studies on vaccine uptake in rural areas”. You send a proposal that follows their template.

Your own push
Unsolicited proposal
Meaning: No one asked you. You send it because you think they may like your idea.
Example

You send a proposal to a tech company to study mental health and their app use among teens, even though they did not post a call.

Short first step
Pre-proposal
Meaning: Very short version (often 1–2 pages or a letter). Used so that the funder can decide if you should send the full version.
Example

You send a one-page concept note about “screen time and sleep”. If they like it, they ask for the full 20-page proposal.

Ongoing work
Continuation & renewal
Continuation: You already got funding for year 1. Now you ask for year 2 or 3, with proof of progress.
Renewal: Project is ending. You ask for a fresh cycle of support for a new phase.
Example

Your community health project finishes its first 3 years. You send a renewal proposal to test new training tools in more clinics.

For most PhD students, you will write: an internal proposal, an external proposal, or a pre-proposal. Always read the exact rules of the place you send it to.

Why this matters

What your proposal must do and how it looks when it is good

Here we blend “purpose”, “characteristics”, “qualities”, and “functions”.
Main job
Three things your proposal must prove
Your text must quietly answer:
  1. Problem is important. It is not a tiny or boring issue.
  2. Plan is clear and realistic. Methods, time, and cost make sense.
  3. You and your team can do it. Skills and past work match the plan.
If any one is weak, your proposal is at risk.
PhD example
Check on our “phone & sleep” example
  • Important? Yes. Sleep and grades matter to the university.
  • Clear plan? Survey + sleep logs + basic stats. Simple and doable in one year.
  • Right person? You show that you know basic statistics and have done a pilot survey.
If you miss one, a reviewer may think: “Nice idea, but not for funding now.”
Look and feel
How a good proposal “feels” when reading
  • Scope is clear. Not too wide, not too tiny.
  • Time and money are realistic. Not fantasy, not vague.
  • Language is plain. No need to guess what you mean.
  • Benefits for funder or field are clear, not hidden.
  • Risks are named honestly with ideas to handle them.
  • Length is enough but not full of empty words.
A good proposal reads like a clear plan from a careful person, not like a sales pitch or a poem.
Why prepare well
Your proposal is your first “exam” as a researcher
  • Funds are limited, so weak proposals are cut quickly.
  • Good proposals help you plan your whole PhD path.
  • Writing forces you to fix fuzzy thinking early.
  • Reviewers can give feedback to improve the study.
Time spent here saves months of pain later in your PhD.

Skeleton

Three big blocks: beginning, middle, end

Every proposal, no matter how long, uses this simple shape.
Beginning = Introduction
  • Shows the problem.
  • Explains who is hurt by this problem.
  • Gives a simple idea of your solution.
  • States main aim and maybe 2–4 clear objectives.
Example line
“This study will examine how late-night phone use affects sleep quality in first-year students at X University.”
Middle = Body
This is where detail lives.
  • Background and literature.
  • Preliminary work you already did (if any).
  • Research methods (design, sample, tools, analysis).
  • Timeline and plan of tasks.
  • Budget and resources.
If the reader is lost here, the proposal fails.
End = Conclusion / Recommendation
  • Reminds the reader of the benefit if they say “yes”.
  • Shows the main expected results or impact.
  • May make a clear ask (“We request X amount for Y years”).
Example lines
“By funding this study, the university will gain data to design better rules on phone use and sleep for first-year students.”

Core parts

Every main part of your proposal, step by step

Open each box. Copy the pattern. Change the content to your own topic.
Part 1 Title Short, clear, under ~10 words if possible.
›

Your goal: Let the reader know at once what you will study, in plain words.

Simple pattern

[Main variable] and [other variable] in [group] at [place]
or
[Short action phrase]: A study in [group / place]

Good examples
  • “Phone Use And Sleep Quality In First-Year Students”
  • “Diet And Blood Sugar Control In Adults With Type 2 Diabetes”
  • “Teacher Burnout In Public Schools In City X”
Weak vs strong
Weak title Problem Better title
“A Study Of Students” Too vague. What study? Which students? “Stress Levels In Final-Year Engineering Students”
“Modern Issues In Health” Too wide. No clear group or variable. “Exercise Habits In Nurses Working Night Shifts”
  • No slang or jokes.
  • Avoid rare acronyms. If you must use one, spell it first in the abstract.
  • Do not try to be cute. Be clear.
Part 2 Abstract Short summary, about 100–200 words.
›

The abstract is a mini-version of your whole proposal. Many reviewers read this first. Some may only read this and skim the rest.

Simple structure
  1. Background: 1–2 lines about the problem.
  2. Aim: 1 line with your main aim.
  3. Methods: 2–3 lines on design, sample, tools, analysis.
  4. Expected results / impact: 1–2 lines.
Example abstract (phone & sleep)

Many first-year students use smartphones late at night, which may harm sleep and learning. This study aims to examine the link between late-night phone use and sleep quality in first-year students at X University. We will conduct a cross-sectional survey of 300 students using a structured questionnaire on phone use and a standard sleep quality scale. Data will be analyzed using descriptive statistics and regression models. The findings are expected to help the university design better student support and awareness programs on healthy phone habits.

  • Write the abstract last, when the rest is clear.
  • Do not include new details that are not in the main text.
  • Do not use references or long quotes here.
Part 3 Introduction Problem, importance, aim, and objectives.
›

Your goal: Make the reader care about the problem and see your clear aim.

  1. Start wide: what general area is this (for example, sleep in students)?
  2. Move to your specific problem (late-night phone use and sleep).
  3. Show why this problem matters (health, learning, cost, etc.).
  4. State your main aim and clear objectives.
Aim & objectives pattern

Aim: To [main action] [main variables] in [group] at [place].

Objectives:
1. To measure …
2. To compare …
3. To find the association between … and …

Example introduction (short)

Smartphone use has become common among university students. Many students use their phones late at night for social media, games, and videos. Late-night phone use may shorten sleep and reduce sleep quality. Poor sleep is linked with low grades, mood problems, and health issues.

Aim: To examine the association between late-night phone use and sleep quality in first-year students at X University.

Objectives:
1. To measure the level of late-night phone use in first-year students.
2. To assess sleep quality in the same group.
3. To study the association between late-night phone use and sleep quality.

  • Keep it focused. Do not turn it into a full literature review.
  • Put numbers only if they help (for example, “60% of students…” with a reference).
Part 4 Background / Literature Show what is already known and what is missing.
›

Here you show that you know the key work in your area, and that there is a gap your study will fill.

Simple flow
  1. What the field knows in general (few key studies).
  2. What is known in your context or country (if any studies exist).
  3. What is missing (gap).
  4. How your study will help fill that gap.
Use recent, good-quality sources. You do not need every paper. Choose the most important ones.
Example gap statement

Several studies in high-income countries have reported a link between late-night phone use and poor sleep among university students. However, little is known about this association in low- and middle-income settings. In country X, there are no published studies on late-night phone use and sleep among first-year university students. This study will address this gap by examining the association between late-night phone use and sleep quality in first-year students at X University.

Part 5 Preliminary work (if you have any) Show what you already tried and learned.
›

If you have done a small pilot or related work, show it briefly here. This proves that your idea is practical and that you can do the study.

Example: pilot survey

A small pilot survey was conducted among 30 first-year students at X University to test the questionnaire. The pilot showed that students understood the items and could complete the survey in less than 10 minutes. Based on the pilot, two items were rephrased to improve clarity. The pilot also suggested that about 70% of students used their phone after 11 pm.

  • Keep this section short and focused.
  • Show only key findings that support your main study.
  • If you have no preliminary work, you can skip this or say “No prior work was done.”
Part 6 Research methodology Design, sample, tools, procedure, analysis, timeline.
›

This is the heart of your proposal. A strong method section answers: what you will do, how you will do it, who you will include, and how you will handle data and problems.

Key parts you must cover
  • Study design (for example, cross-sectional, cohort, experiment).
  • Study setting (where the work happens).
  • Study population and inclusion / exclusion rules.
  • Sample size and how you calculated it.
  • Sampling method (random, convenience, etc.).
  • Data collection tools (questionnaires, lab tests, interviews).
  • Data collection procedure (step-by-step).
  • Data analysis plan (which tests, which software).
  • Timeline.
  • Possible problems and how you will handle them.
Mini method example (phone & sleep)

Design: Cross-sectional survey.
Setting: X University, first-year classes.
Population: All first-year students enrolled in 2026.
Sample size: 300 students. Sample size was calculated using a 95% confidence level, 5% margin of error, and an expected 50% prevalence of late-night phone use.
Sampling: Simple random sampling from the list of first-year students.
Tools: 1) Structured questionnaire on phone use. 2) Standard sleep quality scale.
Data collection: Data will be collected during class time after obtaining consent. Students will complete a paper or online survey in 10–15 minutes.
Analysis: Data will be entered into SPSS. Descriptive statistics will be used to summarize phone use and sleep quality. Chi-square tests and logistic regression will be used to assess the association between late-night phone use and poor sleep quality.
Timeline: Month 1–2: tool design and ethics approval. Month 3–4: data collection. Month 5–6: data analysis and report writing.

Avoid saying only “We will analyze the data”. Always say how you will analyze it and with which tests or methods.
Part 7 Budget Money for people, tools, and support.
›

Your budget shows what the study will cost and why each cost is needed. A clear budget also shows that you understand the real work and do not live in fantasy.

Three main cost types
Type Meaning Examples
Direct costs Money directly used for the study. Assistant salary, printing surveys, lab tests, travel to field sites.
Subcontractor costs Money paid to another group you work with. Partner lab that runs blood tests, data center that stores data.
Indirect costs Institution overhead for general support. Electricity, building use, admin support (set by your institution).
Tiny budget example (numbers are only sample)

Direct costs:
• Data collection assistant (6 months, part-time) – $2,000
• Printing questionnaires and posters – $200
• Gift vouchers for participants (300 × $3) – $900
• Software license (SPSS student license) – $300

Subcontractor costs:
• Not applicable (no external partners).

Indirect costs:
• 10% of total direct costs, as per university policy – $340

Total budget requested: $3,740

  • Include a short note that explains each line (“budget justification”).
  • Follow the format given by the funder or university.
  • Do not ask for items that are clearly not linked to the study.
Part 8 Your CV and team Show that the right people are in place.
›

Many proposals ask for a brief academic CV for the main person and maybe for co-workers.

What to show
  • Your education (degrees, year, place).
  • Past research work or thesis.
  • Skills that match this study (for example, statistics, lab skills).
  • Any papers, posters, or talks.
Mini example

Name: A. Student
Current role: PhD candidate, Department of Public Health, X University.
Education: MPH, 2024, X University.
Experience: Assisted in a cross-sectional study on stress in medical students. Completed a thesis on “Social media use and anxiety in undergraduates”.
Skills: SPSS, survey design, basic regression analysis.

Part 9 Appendix Extra support items only.
›

Use this section only if allowed. It holds extra pieces that support your main text.

  • Letters of support from partners.
  • Permission letters from hospitals or schools.
  • Draft questionnaires or interview guides.
  • Gantt chart or detailed timeline.
Do not hide key information in the appendix. Anything vital (like methods) must also appear in the main body.
Part 10 Ethics and human / animal subjects Show that your work is safe and respectful.
›

If your study involves people or animals, you must explain how you protect them and follow rules.

Key points to include
  • Which ethics body will review the study.
  • How you will get informed consent.
  • How you will keep data private and safe.
  • How you will reduce risk or harm.
  • How you will allow people to stop at any time.
Example ethics paragraph

Ethical approval will be obtained from the Institutional Review Board of X University before data collection. Written informed consent will be taken from all participants. Participation will be voluntary, and students may withdraw at any time without any effect on their grades. No names will be recorded on the questionnaire. Data will be stored in password-protected files, and only the research team will have access.

Planning

Timeline and problem-handling plan

Reviewers like clear time lines and honest risk plans.
Simple timeline (Gantt-style in words)
Months 1–2: Planning
Refine questions, build tools, apply for ethics, pilot test the questionnaire.
Months 3–4: Data collection
Recruit sample, collect surveys, check data quality.
Months 5–6: Analysis
Clean data, run analysis, make tables and graphs.
Months 7–8: Write-up
Write report, draft paper, share with supervisor and team.
Plan for common problems
  • Low response rate: Plan extra reminders and more classes to include.
  • Missing data: Decide how you will handle incomplete forms in advance.
  • Time delays: Keep buffer time in your timeline.
  • Team changes: Name a backup person if one assistant leaves.
Example line in proposal

To manage possible low response, we will send reminders through class representatives and email. We will also plan to invite 20% more students than the required sample size.

Style & layout

How to write so reviewers do not get tired

This covers structure, language, and visual layout.
Use clear structure
  • Use headings and sub-headings (like “3.1 Study design”).
  • Use bullet points instead of long thick paragraphs when possible.
  • Use short sentences. Keep most under 20 words.
Use visuals wisely
  • Tables to show budgets or comparisons.
  • Simple diagrams to show study flow (for example, “Invite → Consent → Survey → Analysis”).
  • Do not add complex charts just to look smart.
Every visual must make reading easier, not harder.
Avoid weak words
  • “We might do…” → say “We will do…”.
  • “It appears that maybe…” → say “We expect that…”.
  • “We hope to consider…” → say “We plan to measure…”.
Before you submit: language and layout test
  • Run spell-check and grammar check.
  • Ask one friend outside your field to read the abstract: if they are confused, fix it.
  • Check that font, spacing, and heading style are the same everywhere.
  • Page numbers are in order, no missing pages.
  • All tables and figures are named and referenced in the text.
  • Follow all formatting rules from the funder or school (margins, font size, word limits).

Red flags

Why good-looking proposals still get rejected

Each red flag with a fix and a short example.
Red flag 1
Problem is too small or not useful

If your question will not change anything important, reviewers may drop it.

  • Bad: “Do students like blue or red pens more?”
  • Better: “Does handwriting tool (pen vs tablet) affect note-taking quality and grades?”
Red flag 2
Aim is unclear or too wide

“To study health in students” is not a clear aim. It is too broad and shows no focus.

  • Fix: Narrow it. “To assess depression levels and related factors in first-year students.”
Red flag 3
Methods do not match aims

You want to test cause and effect but choose a weak design.

  • Bad: Aim: “To test if a new app causes weight loss.” Method: one-time cross-sectional survey.
  • Better: Use a trial design or at least before-after study.
Red flag 4
Weak or no statistics plan

Saying “We will analyze the data” is not enough.

  • Fix: Name tests (for example, chi-square, t-test, regression). Say which variables are dependent / independent.
Red flag 5
Poor writing and errors

Many grammar mistakes, missing pages, or random fonts make reviewers think: “This person may not manage a full study.”

  • Fix: Proof-read, use a simple style, and follow one format from start to end.
Red flag 6
Too many elements in one study

Trying to measure every possible variable in one PhD can sink you.

  • Fix: Focus on a few main variables. You can always plan extra studies later.

Quick tools

Three simple checklists you can tick before submission

Use these as last-minute guards.
Checklist 1: Content
  • Clear title, abstract, introduction, background.
  • Aim and objectives are specific and match methods.
  • Methods fully describe who, where, what, how, when.
  • Budget and timeline are realistic.
  • Ethics and consent are covered where needed.
Checklist 2: Logic
  • Background logically leads to the research gap.
  • Gap clearly leads to aim and objectives.
  • Objectives are answered by your methods.
  • Expected results and impact fit your aims.
Checklist 3: Reader experience
  • Headings guide the reader through the text.
  • No paragraph is longer than half a page.
  • Tables and figures are easy to read and not crowded.
  • No lazy phrases: “etc.”, “and so on”, “many things”.
  • Rules from the funder or university are followed exactly.

Walk-through

From idea to mini proposal: full simple example

Same topic used across parts so you can see the flow.
Topic
Late-night phone use and sleep quality in first-year students
  1. Title: Phone Use And Sleep Quality In First-Year Students At X University.
  2. Abstract (short): You write 100–150 words like the earlier example, touching on problem, aim, methods, expected impact.
  3. Introduction: You explain that phone use is high, sleep is important, and you state your aim and three objectives.
  4. Background: You show a few key papers from other countries, note that your country or university has no such study, and state the gap.
  5. Method: You describe your cross-sectional survey, sample, tools, data collection, and analysis plan.
  6. Budget: You list costs for assistant, printing, software, and small gifts, plus indirect costs if needed.
  7. Ethics: You show how you will protect student privacy and get consent.
  8. Conclusion: You state that results will help the university decide on support programs for healthy phone use and sleep.
Try it with your own topic

Pick your topic now and write one sentence for each part:

  1. My title is: “_____”.
  2. My problem in one line is: “_____”.
  3. My main aim is: “To _____ in _____.”
  4. My main method is: “I will use _____ design with _____ participants.”
  5. My main tools are: “_____ and _____.”
  6. My main analysis is: “I will use _____ tests.”
  7. My main benefit is: “This will help _____ to _____.”

Last step

10-minute final check before you send

Fast but strict.
1. Alignment check
  • Title matches your aim and main variables.
  • Aim and objectives match your methods and analysis.
  • Expected impact matches your problem and target group.
2. Reader test
  • Read your abstract out loud. If you run out of breath, sentence is too long.
  • Check that every page looks clean, with enough margin and spacing.
  • Check that each section starts with a clear heading.
3. Honesty check
  • Is your sample size realistic for your time and budget?
  • Are your skills enough for the planned methods? If not, have you added support from someone?
  • Have you clearly said what could go wrong and how you will respond?
Once you can tick most of the boxes in this guide, your proposal is much closer to a strong “yes”.
